One chat,
one job.
15 minutes

Claude can only see what's in the room.

In the room
Your messageFiles you've uploadedThe Project's instructionsEverything said in this conversation

Not in the room: your other chats, last week's thread, your Drive, your inbox, anything you haven't shown it. Claude is not being difficult. The room is all there is.

What's in the room is called context. Every technique in this session is really one idea: get the right things into the room.

Part 02 · Chat vs Cowork
Right tool,
right job.
20 minutes

One app. Two very different colleagues.

Chat Mode: thinking together

Drafting, research, reviewing, arguing a strategy through. Lives in conversation. This is where your team's shared Projects are, so every chat starts with the client context already loaded.

Cowork Mode: doing the work

Works with the files on your computer, runs skills, sets up scheduled tasks, and produces finished documents rather than chat replies. Local to your machine.

02 · Chat vs Cowork■Guide · 02 Which Interface, When

Talking about the work? Chat.
Producing the work? Cowork.

Draft a tricky client email → Chat Turn 40 files into one report → Cowork Pressure-test a strategy → Chat Build a skill → Cowork Rework a proposal's narrative → Chat "Every Monday, brief me" → Cowork

Not a law, a default. You'll develop a feel for the exceptions within a week.

Part 04 · Prompting
The prompt
is the brief.
15 minutes

"Make it pop" is not a brief.

If a client sent your team a one-line email and expected finished work, you'd push back. You'd ask about audience, objectives, tone, format, deadline.

A lazy prompt is that one-line email. Claude won't push back unless you ask it to. It will just do its best with nothing, and its best with nothing is mediocre.

04 · Prompting■Guide · 07 Prompt Principles

Every good prompt has three parts.

Context

What Claude needs to know: the client, the situation, the background, the audience.

Task

Exactly what you want done. One job, stated plainly. Not three jobs in a trench coat.

Constraints

Format, length, tone, what to avoid. The difference between "a draft" and "your draft".

Context, task, constraints. Same framework as your guide, same order every time.

04 · Prompting■Guide · 07 Prompt Principles
Live Demo

Habits that compound.

  • Front-load. Everything relevant in the first message. Ten follow-up corrections cost more than one proper brief.
  • One job per message. Bundled requests get bundled quality.
  • On anything big, end with: "Ask me questions before you start." Claude interviews you, and the output jumps a level.
  • Going in circles? Stop. New chat, better brief, first time.

Part 06 · Staying safe
Confidence is
not accuracy.
10 minutes

Claude fails in four predictable ways.

It invents specifics

Names, statistics, references and prices that sound right and don't exist. Delivered fluently, which is the dangerous part.

It agrees with you too easily

Frame a bad idea warmly and Claude finds merit in it. Ask it to argue against you instead.

It fills gaps silently

Missing information doesn't stop it. It assumes, and doesn't always say so.

Long conversations drift

Instructions from message five fade by message forty. You know this one already: one chat, one job.

06 · Staying safe■Guide · 10 Where Claude Goes Wrong
Guide Moment

One habit catches most of it.

Before anything goes to a client, run the verification prompt from your guide on the draft:

Review the draft above as a fact-checker. List every factual claim, statistic, name and reference I should verify before this goes to a client. Then flag anywhere you were uncertain or filled a gap with an assumption rather than something I told you.

Claude drafts, you decide. That rule never changes, no matter how good the drafts get.
